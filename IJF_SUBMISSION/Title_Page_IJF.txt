ABSTRACT:

We systematically review 66 empirical studies (2007–2025) examining sentiment-based 
economic forecasting methods, validation practices, and performance. Meta-analysis 
establishes median RMSE improvement of 20% (range 5–42%) over benchmark models across 
domains: 18% for GDP, 22% for inflation, 28% for volatility. However, validation 
practices vary substantially: 83% employ out-of-sample testing, 71% use Diebold-Mariano 
tests, but only 33% follow real-time data protocols (critical for practical deployment). 
We document methodological evolution from dictionary-based methods (55% of studies) 
through traditional ML (6%) to BERT/transformers (6%) and LLMs (2%), characterizing 
trade-offs: interpretability vs. accuracy vs. cost. We develop a best-practices validation 
framework (Tier 1: Essential; Tier 2: Recommended; Tier 3: Advanced) and identify common 
pitfalls practitioners should avoid: real-time protocol violations (~35% of papers), 
weak baseline selection, inadequate robustness checks. Geographic analysis reveals 56% 
of studies focus on USA, 84% use English text, zero coverage of Africa/Latin America. 
For practitioners deploying sentiment forecasting systems, we provide actionable decision 
trees and recommendations by use case (short-term vs. long-term, exploratory vs. 
production). Core finding: sentiment indices provide economically meaningful forecast 
improvements but require rigorous validation discipline for real-world reliability.
