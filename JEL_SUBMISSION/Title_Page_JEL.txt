ABSTRACT:

Despite 18 years of research on sentiment-based economic forecasting, 
fundamental questions remain unresolved. We critically examine 66 empirical 
studies (2007–2025), identifying three key unresolved tensions: (1) 
causality ambiguity—does sentiment cause outcomes or merely reveal them 
earlier? Current evidence relies on Granger causality; no causal identification 
exists. (2) Information vs. noise—are sentiment-driven movements persistent 
or reverting? Evidence suggests context-dependent distinction: informative 
during crises, noisy during normal times. (3) Geographic and linguistic 
bias—56% of studies focus on USA, 84% analyze English text, zero coverage 
of Africa/Latin America/Bangla speakers. Meta-analysis shows median 20% 
RMSE improvement (range 5–42%), but 35% of papers violate real-time data 
protocols, likely inflating estimates by 1–3%. We develop a best-practices 
validation framework (Tier 1/2/3) and propose research roadmap prioritizing 
causal identification, geographic expansion, and theoretical integration of 
sentiment into structural economic models. The field has achieved empirical 
success in forecasting but lacks theoretical maturity and geographic reach.
