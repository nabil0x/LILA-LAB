Editor, Journal of Economic Literature

Dear Editor,

We submit: "Economic Narratives and Sentiment-Based Forecasting: A 
Critical Assessment of Evidence, Unresolved Questions, and Research Gaps 
(2007–2025)"

This systematic review synthesizes 66 studies with explicit focus on 
UNRESOLVED QUESTIONS in the sentiment-based forecasting literature, 
addressing a critical gap that JEL targets: identifying and articulating 
what remains unknown in economics.

THREE UNRESOLVED TENSIONS:

1. THE CAUSALITY QUESTION (Section 3.3):
   Current evidence: 55/66 papers show sentiment Granger-causes outcomes
   PROBLEM: Granger causality ≠ causal effect
   
   Example: Does media pessimism cause GDP decline, or does expected 
   GDP decline cause media pessimism?
   
   Current state: Only Hansen & McMahon (2016) use quasi-exogenous FOMC 
   shocks. No instrumental variables. No natural experiments.
   
   Implications: Practitioners cannot infer whether sentiment management 
   (central bank communication) causally influences real outcomes. Policy 
   effectiveness remains unknown.

2. THE INFORMATION VS. NOISE DEBATE (Section 3.2):
   Tetlock (2007): Media pessimism predicts returns 1-5 days ahead, 
   then reversals (noise, overreaction).
   
   Da et al. (2015): FEARS index predicts returns 2-3 weeks ahead 
   (information, genuine predictive power).
   
   PROBLEM: Both interpretations fit the same data. Context appears to 
   matter: crisis periods show information; normal periods show noise. 
   But this distinction lacks formal theory.

3. THE GEOGRAPHIC AND LINGUISTIC GAP (Section 4.5):
   56% USA, 84% English, 0% Africa/Latin America/Bangla
   
   CONSEQUENCE: We know sentiment works in developed English-speaking 
   markets. We have no evidence for:
   - Emerging markets with sparse macro data (where sentiment could be 
     most valuable)
   - Non-English language economies (Asia, Africa, South America)
   - Crisis periods in developing countries
   
   RESEARCH OPPORTUNITY: Bangladesh Bank could deploy BanglaBERT for 
   real-time inflation sentiment within 24 hours—but no precedent exists.

NOVEL CONTRIBUTIONS FOR JEL:

✓ IDENTIFICATION OF UNRESOLVED QUESTIONS (JEL core mission):
  Not "what we know" but "what we don't know" and why it matters

✓ CRITICAL ASSESSMENT:
  35% of papers violate real-time data protocols → estimates inflated 1-3%
  Only 18% use advanced statistical tests (MCS/SPA)
  Publication bias likely: null results underrepresented

✓ THEORETICAL GAPS:
  No formal theory of when sentiment is information vs. noise
  Causality identification nearly absent
  Sentiment rarely embedded in structural macro models

✓ RESEARCH ROADMAP:
  Priority 1: Causal identification using natural experiments/IV
  Priority 2: Geographic expansion to emerging markets
  Priority 3: Integration into DSGE/structural models

RELEVANCE TO JEL AUDIENCE:

JEL publishes comprehensive literature surveys that identify gaps and 
guide future research. This review does exactly that: it identifies 
three fundamental unresolved questions and proposes a research agenda.

The geographic gap (zero Bangla, zero Africa) is significant: 4 billion 
people live in these regions, yet we have zero economic sentiment indices.

ORIGINALITY:

This is the first systematic review of sentiment forecasting with 
explicit focus on unresolved questions and research gaps. Previous 
surveys emphasized empirical success; this emphasizes what remains unknown.

The identification of the "real-time protocol gap" (35% of papers) is 
novel and consequential: it implies published improvements are inflated.
