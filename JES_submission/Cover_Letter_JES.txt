WHY THIS REVIEW MATTERS

Sentiment-based economic forecasting has grown from a niche curiosity
(Tetlock, 2007) to a rapidly expanding field employing diverse methodologies
from dictionary lexicons to large language models. Despite 18 years and 66+
peer-reviewed studies, no systematic review has consolidated the evidence on
forecast performance, methodological trade-offs, and validation practices.
Our review fills this gap through rigorous meta-analysis, structured quality
assessment, and explicit documentation of geographic and linguistic biases.

FOUR KEY CONTRIBUTIONS

1. COMPREHENSIVE META-ANALYSIS (Section 5)
   We synthesize 66 studies spanning four methodological eras and establish:
   - Median 20% RMSE reduction across all domains
   - Domain-specific estimates: GDP 18%, Inflation 22%, Volatility 28%
   - Evidence grading system for evaluating confidence in findings
   - Robustness checks confirming results hold for high-quality studies

2. METHODOLOGICAL TAXONOMY (Section 4)
   We provide the first systematic comparison of all four sentiment extraction
   approaches: Dictionary (55% of studies), Traditional ML (6%),
   BERT/Transformers (6%), and LLMs (3%), with explicit trade-offs in
   accuracy, interpretability, cost, and scalability.

3. VALIDATION BEST-PRACTICES FRAMEWORK (Section 8)
   We develop a tiered framework (Essential/Recommended/Advanced) for
   sentiment forecasting validation, documenting that while 83% of studies
   use out-of-sample testing, only 33% follow real-time data protocols and
   only 18% employ advanced statistical tests (MCS/SPA). This framework is
   immediately actionable for researchers and practitioners.

4. QUANTIFIED GEOGRAPHIC AND LINGUISTIC GAPS (Section 6)
   We provide the first systematic documentation of research concentration:
   56% North America, 84% English, 0% Africa/Latin America/Bangla. The Bangla Economic
   Narrative Index (BENI) case study demonstrates a concrete pathway for
   expanding sentiment analysis to the 265 million Bangla speakers -- the
   world's 7th most spoken language with zero economic sentiment indices.
