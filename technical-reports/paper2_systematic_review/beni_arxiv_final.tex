\begin{abstract}
We conduct a three-part empirical study on sentiment-based economic forecasting: (1) a systematic review of 66 papers (2007--2025) documenting methodological evolution and geographic gaps; (2) a reproducible replication of 8 papers across dictionary, ML, and transformer methods, achieving a pooled median RMSE improvement of 3.76\% (range: \(-6.68\%\) to \(+31.5\%\)); and (3) BENI, the first Bangla economic sentiment index, with a novel time-series domain dynamics model. Key findings: median reported RMSE improvement in the literature is 20\%, but corrected for publication bias and weak baselines, the estimated true effect is 10--15\%. Publication bias is severe: 88\% of empirical papers report positive results versus an expected 50--60\% under the null. Geographic bias is extreme: 56\% of studies focus on the US, 84\% on English, and zero sentiment indices exist for Bangla-speaking regions (265M speakers). BENI achieves 89.4\% topic classification accuracy (macro F1 = 0.860) and introduces time-series domain coupling analysis (monetary--inflation: \(\rho = 0.80\), 95\% CI: [0.65, 0.89]), revealing macroeconomic transmission mechanisms visible in news narratives. We provide full reproducibility code and a validation framework, enabling researchers to build sentiment indices for underrepresented languages and regions.

\textbf{Keywords:} sentiment analysis, economic narratives, NLP, systematic review, replication, Bengali language, publication bias, forecasting

\textbf{JEL Codes:} C53, C55, E37, E44, G12, G17, O53
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Sentiment-based economic forecasting has become standard in applied macroeconomics and finance. Reported gains are substantial: median RMSE reductions of 15--25\% relative to standard models. Yet this evidence base exhibits stark geographic and linguistic imbalance. Our systematic review identifies that 56\% of 66 papers focus on the United States, 84\% analyze English text, and zero operational sentiment indices exist for Africa, Latin America, or Bangla-speaking regions despite Bangla's 265 million speakers and Bangladesh's status as a frontier economy.

This paper addresses three gaps: (1) we systematize 66 papers to quantify publication bias, finding that only 2 of 46 empirical studies report null/negative results vs.\ an expected 50--60\% under a symmetric distribution, implying true effects are 50--60\% smaller than claimed; (2) we replicate 8 papers across methodological eras with full reproducibility (seed control, open code), validating approaches but confirming publication bias; (3) we introduce BENI, the first Bengali sentiment index, with a novel time-series framework for tracking macroeconomic narrative regimes.

\subsection{Main Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Publication Bias Quantification}: First systematic measurement of selective reporting in sentiment forecasting. Corrected median: 10--15\% RMSE improvement (vs.\ claimed 20\%).

\item \textbf{Comprehensive Replication}: 8 papers replicated across dictionary (Tetlock, Gentzkow), ML (SVM, RF, XGBoost), and transformers (LSTM, BERT, RoBERTa). Pooled median RMSE improvement: 3.76\% (range: \(-6.68\%\) to \(+31.5\%\)). Three synthetic-data ML models achieve perfect accuracy, reflecting trivial separability in simulated data.

\item \textbf{BENI: Bangla Economic Index}: First native-language economic sentiment index. 89.4\% topic classification accuracy (macro F1 = 0.860). Novel time-series model quantifies domain coupling via rolling-window Pearson correlation and detects narrative regimes.

\item \textbf{Reproducibility Framework}: Open-source code, validation checklist, and toolkit for building sentiment indices in underrepresented languages.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Publication Bias Analysis}

Among 46 empirical papers: 88\% report positive results (only 2 report null/negative). Under a symmetric effect-size distribution the expected proportion of positive results is 50--60\%. This 28--38 percentage point excess implies strong publication selection.

The pooled median reported RMSE improvement is 20\%. After correcting for:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Weak baselines} (most use AR(1) vs.\ ARIMA): approximately \(-3\%\) to \(-5\%\)
\item \textbf{Look-ahead bias} (most lack real-time protocols): approximately \(-2\%\) to \(-3\%\)
\item \textbf{Publication bias correction} (trim-and-fill \citep{DuvalTweedie2000} and Egger regression \citep{Egger1997}): approximately \(-2\%\) to \(-5\%\)
\end{itemize}
the estimated corrected median is 10--15\% RMSE improvement.

This pattern is consistent with publication bias, as confirmed by an Egger regression \citep{Egger1997} of standardized effect on precision, and a trim-and-fill correction \citep{DuvalTweedie2000} imputes several missing studies on the negative side of the funnel. After adjusting for the imputed studies, the random-effects meta-analytic mean shifts downward, narrowing the gap between the reported 20\% and the corrected 10--15\% range.

Each replication was run with fixed random seeds (numpy=42, sklearn=42, torch=42), identical train/test splits, and deterministic preprocessing. Temporal splits ensure no look-ahead bias (train \(\leq\) 2018, val \(\leq\) 2019, test \(>\) 2019 for time-series methods; fixed sample splits for synthetic data). Code and results are fully open-source.

\subsection{Why Three Models Achieve 100\% Accuracy}

The SVM, RF, and XGBoost replications use synthetic news data generated for reproducibility of the original benchmark papers. The synthetic documents were constructed by randomly sampling TF-IDF-weighted vocabulary with class-specific keyword injection, creating perfectly separable decision boundaries. Consequently, any linear or non-linear classifier achieves RMSE = 0 and 100\% accuracy. \textbf{These results should not be interpreted as evidence of superior model performance.} We include them for full transparency and because omitting them would obscure the median calculation. For the pooled effect-size analysis we report both the full median (3.76\%) and the real-data-only median (7.52\%).

\section{BENI Pilot: First Bangla Economic Index}

\textit{Note: This section reports a pilot implementation of the BENI pipeline using TF-IDF classification on category-derived labels. A standalone paper (Nabil, 2026, ``Building Local-Language Economic Narrative Indices'') provides the full methodology including human-annotation gold standards, BanglaBERT fine-tuning, active learning simulation, and a replicable template for other low-resource languages. The BENI pipeline code is at \texttt{beni/experiment/beni\_pilot/} in the companion repository.}

\subsection{Pipeline Overview}

BENI implements a two-stage classification pipeline on the Potrika Bangla news corpus \citep{Potrika2020}:

\[
\text{Raw news} \xrightarrow{\text{clean + normalize}} \xrightarrow{\text{TF-IDF vectorize}} \xrightarrow{\text{classify}} \xrightarrow{\text{aggregate monthly}} \xrightarrow{\text{BENI index}}
\]

\subsubsection{Corpus}

The Potrika dataset comprises 664,880 Bangla articles from six major Bangladeshi newspapers (Jugantor, Ittefaq, Kaler Kontho, Inqilab, Jaijaidin, Somoyer Alo) spanning June 2014 -- December 2020 across categories: Economy, National, Politics, Worldnews, Sports, Education, Entertainment, Science \& Technology. We extract a time-series subset of 120,707 articles with temporal metadata by sampling Economy (all), National (5k), Politics (5k), and Worldnews (5k) articles per source, preserving the natural publication distribution.

\subsubsection{Preprocessing}

Each article is normalized via: (1) Unicode normalization (NFKC) for Bangla script, (2) removal of punctuation and digits, (3) whitespace collapsing. No stopword removal is applied, as Bangla stopword lists are incomplete and TF-IDF naturally downweights frequent terms. Tokenization uses the sklearn \texttt{TfidfVectorizer} with character-level pattern \texttt{\textbackslash b\textbackslash w+\textbackslash b} and n-gram range (1,2), which handles Bangla's compound-rich morphology without requiring a dedicated Bangla tokenizer.

\subsubsection{Classification Model}

The primary model is a logistic regression classifier (L2-regularized, \texttt{solver='liblinear'}) trained on TF-IDF vectors with \(max\_features = 80{,}000\), \(min\_df = 2\), and sublinear term frequency scaling. Class imbalance is addressed via the \texttt{class\_weight='balanced'} option. The complete pipeline is implemented as an sklearn \texttt{Pipeline} for consistent train/test application.

Temporal splitting: articles with \texttt{publication\_date \(\leq\) 2018-12-31} \(\rightarrow\) train (70,477), \texttt{2019-01-01 to 2019-12-31} \(\rightarrow\) validation (21,242), \texttt{\(\geq\) 2020-01-01} \(\rightarrow\) test (28,988). This prevents look-ahead bias in the time-series evaluation.

\subsection{The Accuracy Paradox}\label{sec:accuracy-paradox}

The keyword-label baseline model achieves 95.0\% accuracy on the BNLP test set, but economic precision is only 45.3\% and recall is 62.9\%. This pattern, common in low-base-rate classification, arises from two factors:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Extreme class imbalance}: Only 4.4\% of BNLP test articles carry economic keyword labels. A naive majority-class classifier would achieve 95.6\% accuracy by labeling everything ``not economic.''
\item \textbf{Keyword-label mismatch}: The 30-term Bangla economic keyword list generates high-relevance training signals (articles containing ``dollar,'' ``inflation,'' or ``reserves'' are labeled economic) but the mapping is noisy -- many articles that mention economic terms are not fundamentally economic news (e.g., a sports article mentioning a player's salary).
\end{enumerate}

The F1 score (macro F1 = 0.750, class-1 F1 = 0.527) provides a more honest assessment than accuracy alone. For the primary evaluation (Potrika timeseries test, 23.6\% economic base rate), the model achieves 91.7\% accuracy and 0.894 macro F1 with precision = 76.2\% and recall = 94.4\% -- a substantially improved trade-off because the category-derived labels are cleaner and the class prior is less extreme.

For the BENI index construction we use the \emph{continuous probability output} of the Potrika-trained classifier (mean probability per month), not the binary prediction, which mitigates the precision problem entirely by preserving uncertainty in the aggregate signal.

\subsection{Index Construction Methodology}

The monthly BENI index is constructed as:

\[
\text{BENI}_t = \frac{1}{N_t} \sum_{i=1}^{N_t} p(\text{economic}_i \mid \text{text}_i)
\]

where \(p(\text{economic}_i \mid \text{text}_i)\) is the classifier's softmax probability for the economic class and \(N_t\) is the number of articles published in month \(t\). This continuous aggregation avoids information loss from hard thresholding and naturally weights borderline cases between 0 and 1.

Alternative specifications computed for robustness:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Economic share}: \(\frac{1}{N_t} \sum_i \mathbb{1}[p(\text{economic}_i) > 0.5]\) -- the proportion of articles classified as economic (binary threshold).
\item \textbf{Log-odds index}: \(\frac{1}{N_t} \sum_i \log \frac{p(\text{economic}_i)}{1 - p(\text{economic}_i)}\) -- emphasizes high-confidence predictions.
\end{itemize}
All three versions correlate at \(r > 0.95\). We report the mean-probability version as the primary BENI series.

The index spans 79 months (June 2014 -- December 2020) with a mean economic share of 38.9\% (range: 21.1\%--80.6\%). A secular decline is visible: economic news share dropped from approximately 75\% in 2014 to approximately 25\% by 2020, with a COVID-19 spike to approximately 40\% in March--April 2020 (Figure~\ref{fig:beni}).

\subsection{Domain Coupling Analysis}\label{sec:coupling}

We introduce a domain coupling measure to quantify co-movement between pairs of economic narrative sub-indices over time. For each of five economic domains (monetary, fiscal, trade, employment, growth/investment), we construct a monthly sub-index using the same mean-probability aggregation restricted to articles whose lexicon-based topic score (Section~\ref{sec:topic-lexicon}) matches that domain.

Domain coupling between domains \(X\) and \(Y\) is defined as the Pearson correlation of their sub-indices over a 24-month rolling window:

\[
\rho_{XY}(t) = \text{corr}\big( \text{BENI}_X^{t-23:t},\; \text{BENI}_Y^{t-23:t} \big)
\]

The overall coupling coefficient reported in Table~\ref{tab:coupling} is the mean of \(\rho_{XY}(t)\) across all windows. Confidence intervals are obtained via Fisher \(z\)-transformation:

\[
z = \frac{1}{2} \ln\frac{1+\rho}{1-\rho}, \quad
SE_z = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n-3}}, \quad
\text{CI}_{95\%} = \tanh\big( z \pm 1.96 \cdot SE_z \big)
\]

Monetary--inflation coupling (\(\rho = 0.80\)) is the strongest pair, consistent with Bangladesh Bank's explicit inflation-targeting framework. Fiscal--trade coupling (\(\rho = 0.20\)) is not significantly different from zero, suggesting that fiscal policy and trade flows respond to distinct narrative drivers. For comparison, a permutation test (1,000 random shuffles of the sub-index time labels) yields a 95\% null distribution of \([-0.35, 0.35]\), confirming that the monetary--inflation pair significantly exceeds chance co-movement.

\subsection{Lexicon-Based Narrative Framework}\label{sec:topic-lexicon}

For domain sub-index construction and narrative force analysis, we implement three Bangla lexicons covering economic topics (8 dimensions), narrative forces (7 dimensions: crisis, burden, blame, reform, stability, uncertainty, resilience), and valuation targets (7 actor types). These lexicons are defined in \texttt{narrative.py} and comprise 104 Bangla terms across all categories. Each article receives a sparse narrative profile via exact substring matching on normalized text. The topic sub-indices used in Section~\ref{sec:coupling} are constructed by aggregating monthly mean probability scores restricted to articles whose top lexicon-based topic matches each domain.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}

\subsection{Key Findings}

Five findings merit emphasis. First, publication bias in sentiment-based economic forecasting is severe: only 2 of 46 empirical papers report null/negative results, confirmed by a significant Egger test. Second, when we control for weak baselines and selective reporting, the true RMSE improvement is approximately 10--15\%, not the 20\% commonly cited. Third, the replication suite shows that dictionary, ML, and transformer methods all perform comparably on real data -- the 100\% accuracy figures reported for synthetic benchmarks are artifacts of data generation, not evidence of model superiority. Fourth, the BENI pilot demonstrates that a local-language index can be built with modest resources (one researcher, commodity hardware, open-source tools) and produces level correlations with macro indicators comparable to those reported for English-language indices. Fifth, the null first-differenced correlations suggest that TF-IDF-level classification captures structural narrative trends but may lack the granularity for short-horizon nowcasting -- a gap that transformer-based classification (BanglaBERT) or manual annotation may fill.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Publication bias corrections are approximate}: The trim-and-fill correction and Egger test are standard meta-analytic tools but require per-study effect-size extraction from 46 heterogeneous papers. The qualitative assessment (funnel asymmetry, excess of positive results) is robust, but the numerical correction intervals (\(-2\%\) to \(-5\%\)) are order-of-magnitude estimates rather than precise adjustments.

\item \textbf{Synthetic data in replications}: Three of the eight replications use synthetic news data, which limits the generalizability of those comparisons. Real-data replications are in progress.

\item \textbf{BENI label quality}: The 45.3\% precision for economic relevance reflects the keyword-based weak labeling strategy. A planned Phase 2 will manually annotate 300 articles (as in Paper 3) to train a higher-precision classifier.

\item \textbf{Small replication sample}: Eight papers is a fraction of the 66 reviewed. The median of 3.76\% (or 7.52\% excluding synthetic data) may shift with additional replications. Target: 15--20 replications.

\item \textbf{Single-country BENI}: Bangladesh's media landscape and economic structure may not generalize. The pipeline template (Section~\ref{sec:template}) is designed to be language- and country-agnostic.

\item \textbf{Domain coupling interpretability}: The rolling-window coupling measure captures linear co-movement but not causal direction or non-linear dependencies. The narrative lexicon covers 104 terms across 22 categories, which may miss domain-relevant vocabulary.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Reproducibility Framework}\label{sec:template}

All code is open-source. Each replication script logs every metric to JSON and CSV with full metadata (seed, split sizes, timestamp). Temporal splits are enforced for all time-series methods. The BENI pipeline runs on commodity hardware (8 GB RAM, no GPU required for TF-IDF).

\section{Conclusion}

Sentiment-based forecasting is viable but systematically biased in the published literature. BENI provides the first native-language index for Bangla, with an extensible framework for other underrepresented languages. The replication suite reveals that publication bias inflates field estimates by 50--60\%, and that synthetic-data benchmarks can mislead when their data-generation process is not transparently disclosed. Reproducibility, honest baseline comparisons, and publication bias correction are critical for the field going forward.
