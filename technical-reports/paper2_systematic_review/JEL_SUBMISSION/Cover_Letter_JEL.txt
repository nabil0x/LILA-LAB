Editor, Journal of Economic Literature

Dear Editor,

We submit: "Sentiment, Narratives, and Economic Outcomes: Unresolved
Questions and a Research Agenda"

This manuscript identifies and formalizes three fundamental unresolved
tensions in sentiment-based economic forecasting—precisely the type of
critical synthesis that JEL's readership expects. Rather than cataloguing
what is known (the approach of existing surveys), we organize the
literature around what remains unknown and why it matters.

THREE UNRESOLVED TENSIONS:

1. THE CAUSALITY QUESTION:
   55/66 papers report Granger causality from sentiment to economic
   outcomes. Yet Granger causality does not identify causal effects.
   Does media pessimism cause GDP decline—or does expected GDP decline
   cause media pessimism? The literature has not distinguished these
   empirically. We formalize the identification problem (Section 3.4)
   and show that the entire literature's central claim—"sentiment drives
   economic outcomes"—rests on correlational evidence insufficient for
   policy guidance.

2. INFORMATION VS. NOISE:
   Tetlock (2007): sentiment predicts returns 1-5 days ahead, then
   reversals (noise/overreaction interpretation).
   Da et al. (2015): FEARS index predicts returns 2-3 weeks ahead
   (information/genuine predictive power interpretation).
   
   Both interpretations fit the data. The field lacks a formal theory
   specifying when sentiment reflects fundamental information versus
   transient noise. Our review suggests context matters (crisis periods
   show information; normal periods show noise)—but this remains a
   conjecture, not a finding.

3. THE GEOGRAPHIC AND LINGUISTIC GAP:
   56% USA, 84% English, 0% Africa/Latin America/Bangla.
   
   The populations most likely to benefit from sentiment-based economic
   monitoring—emerging markets with sparse real-time macro data—are
   entirely absent from the evidence base. This is simultaneously a
   limitation and the field's largest research opportunity.

NOVEL CONTRIBUTIONS FOR JEL:

✓ FIELD-DEFINING FRAMEWORK:
  Organizes 18 years of research around unresolved tensions rather
  than empirical results—fundamentally different from existing surveys.

✓ CAUSAL IDENTIFICATION FORMALIZATION:
  The first treatment of the identification problem in sentiment
  economics (Section 3.4), including IV, DiD, and RDD frameworks
  with explicit recommendations for natural experiments.

✓ RESEARCH PROGRAM BLUEPRINT:
  Converts the Bangla Economic Narrative Index from a suggestion to
  a concrete research proposal with testable hypotheses (H1-H4),
  methodology, and policy applications.

✓ 10-YEAR RESEARCH AGENDA:
  Phased agenda (Phase I: Geographic expansion + causal identification;
  Phase II: Theoretical integration into DSGE/agent-based models;
  Phase III: Production systems for central banks).

RELEVANCE TO JEL AUDIENCE:

JEL publishes comprehensive surveys that reshape how economists think
about a field. This manuscript does not merely summarize—it identifies
what the field has missed, formalizes why it matters, and provides a
roadmap for the next decade. The geographic gap (4 billion people with
zero sentiment monitoring) is the kind of observation JEL readers find
field-redefining.

ORIGINALITY:
No existing survey organizes sentiment-based economic research around
unresolved tensions with formalized identification problems. Previous
surveys (Kearney & Liu 2014; Gentzkow et al. 2019; Algorithmic
Transparency surveys) catalogued methods; this one identifies what the
field does not know and shows how to find out.
