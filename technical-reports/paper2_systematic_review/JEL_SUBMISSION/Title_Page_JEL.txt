ABSTRACT:

Despite 18 years of research on sentiment-based economic forecasting,
three fundamental questions remain unresolved. First, does sentiment
cause economic outcomes or merely predict them? The literature
overwhelmingly relies on Granger causality—which identifies temporal
ordering, not causal effects. We formalize the identification problem
and show no study satisfies criteria for causal inference. Second, is
sentiment information or noise? The same empirical patterns support
both interpretations; the field lacks a formal theory specifying
conditions under which sentiment reflects fundamentals versus transient
overreaction. Third, whose economy is being studied? Geographic coverage
is heavily concentrated (56% USA, 84% English, 0% Africa/Latin America),
raising fundamental questions about external validity. We propose a
unified research program centered on the Bangla Economic Narrative
Index as a blueprint, with testable hypotheses, causal identification
strategies, and a phased 10-year agenda spanning geographic expansion,
theoretical integration, and central bank adoption. The next decade
will determine whether sentiment-based economic analysis addresses
these tensions or remains a promising but unfulfilled research program.
